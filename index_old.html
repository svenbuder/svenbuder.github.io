Astronomy research

The Milky Way is a uniquely detailed laboratory for galaxy evolution: we can measure stellar ages, orbits, and multi-element chemistry star by star. My work uses this resolved fossil record to test how disc galaxies assemble and enrich themselves with heavy elements, and to connect those signatures to origins through cosmological simulations.

Theme 1: Galaxy evolution via the Milky Way and its analogues

Core question: How did galaxies like the Milky Way build their discs and sustain star formation across cosmic time? I address this by combining chemodynamical survey data with cosmological simulations, using the Milky Way as a benchmark for disc formation, merger history, and the survival of stellar populations.

Chemo-dynamics as a test of disc formation

I use spatially resolved chemistry and kinematics to test disc growth, radial mixing, and the imprint of past events on today’s Milky Way. Example: local variations in radial metallicity gradients and implications for extragalactic inference.

Accretion, mergers, and the fossil record

I developed chemical diagnostics that identify accreted populations and connect them to merger events, combining chemistry with orbits and ages to test formation scenarios.

Simulation tests with Milky Way analogues

I compare cosmological simulations with survey measurements to quantify how major mergers shape discs and halos and to assess how unique the Milky Way’s history may have been.

Selected papers: Buder et al. (2024), Buder et al. (2022), Buder et al. (2025a). Ongoing: NIHAO-UHD merger-memory manuscripts (2025c, 2025d).

Theme 2: The origin of elements

Core question: Where and how are the elements forged? I contribute by producing and validating large, homogeneous multi-element abundance data sets (GALAH), and by pushing toward high-precision elemental (and, where feasible, isotopic) measurements with high-resolution spectroscopy.

What I have delivered

  • Survey-scale abundance mapping: large, homogeneous catalogues that enable population-level constraints on enrichment.
  • Robust spectral inference: careful modelling, calibration, and systematics control for reliable multi-element patterns.
  • Community data products: public releases that support broad use beyond a single science case.

Where I want to go next

  • Elemental and isotopic precision: leveraging high-resolution, stable pipelines to resolve subtle signatures.
  • Binary evolution and mixing: quantifying how binaries and internal processes alter surface abundances.
  • Fast inference of enrichment channels: extracting nucleosynthetic fingerprints from multi-element data at scale.
Selected releases and papers: Buder et al. (2025b) GALAH DR4, Buder et al. (2021) GALAH DR3, Buder et al. (2018) GALAH DR2, Buder et al. (2019) GALAH+ TGAS.
Bridge to Theme 3: Many core challenges in Themes 1–2 (calibration stability, feature attribution, fast inference, and validation) are shared across spectroscopy domains. See Theme 3 for how I am interested in translating these methods beyond astronomy.

Why spectra matter

Spectra encode atomic and molecular fingerprints. Two objects can have similar colours, yet very different absorption-line strengths and chemical compositions. Spectroscopy turns that information into quantitative measurements.

Theme 3: Spectroscopy across disciplines

Where else can the tools developed for stellar spectroscopy be applied to benefit science and society? I am increasingly interested in interdisciplinary spectroscopy that bridges wavelength domains and research communities. My main track record is in astronomy, but I aim to translate robust spectral inference methods—calibration, feature attribution, uncertainty, and scalable pipelines—across disciplines.

Important note: I am not presenting myself as an established interdisciplinary spectroscopy specialist. This is a direction I am actively building, and I welcome collaborators who want to co-develop and stress-test methods across domains.

Concrete ideas I am keen to pursue

  • Feature attribution across domains: identify which wavelength regions carry information for specific properties, and quantify stability across instruments and conditions.
  • Fast, data-driven analysis: adapt astronomy-style machine-learning spectral inference (validated and regularised) for high-throughput applications.
  • High-resolution isotope spectroscopy: extract subtle isotopic or fine-structure signatures where blending, line shapes, and calibration stability are limiting factors.
  • Domain shift: build models that remain reliable when acquisition conditions or sample populations change.

Work that connects well

  • Large-scale spectral pipelines: reproducible, testable workflows from raw data to calibrated spectra to measurements.
  • Data-driven stellar spectroscopy: learning empirical label–flux mappings with careful validation and suppression of unphysical correlations.
  • Pilot cross-domain exploration: early work applying astronomy-style data-driven analysis to leaf reflectance spectroscopy as a proof of concept for broader translation.

If you would like to connect, a helpful first message includes: spectral modality (reflectance / transmission / emission), wavelength range and resolution, target properties, and the main bottleneck (calibration, ground truth, speed, interpretability, or uncertainty).

For students

I supervise projects at multiple levels (Honours, PhD, and visitor projects). Projects often combine three components: (1) scientific questions, (2) real data sets, and (3) modern analysis and reproducible software.

What you will learn

  • How to turn spectra into measurements (calibration, modelling, validation).
  • How to work with large data sets (Python, statistics, uncertainty, pipelines).
  • How to communicate results (figures, writing, talks, reproducibility).

Who should reach out

  • Astronomy students interested in the Milky Way, surveys, or simulations.
  • Students from physics, data science, or engineering who want to work with spectroscopy.
  • Interdisciplinary students interested in translating spectral methods to Earth applications.

About

Academic summary

I am a Galactic archaeologist and stellar astrophysicist. I use stellar spectra to measure detailed chemical abundances and combine them with stellar ages and orbits to reconstruct the Milky Way’s evolution. I completed my PhD in 2019 at the Max Planck Institute for Astronomy (Heidelberg) on combining Gaia kinematics with high-resolution spectroscopy from GALAH. At the Australian National University, I contribute to survey-scale spectroscopy and to methods that enable precision chemo-dynamics, with a focus on how major mergers shaped the Milky Way.
